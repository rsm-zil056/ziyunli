Introduction

In April 1992, New Jersey raised its minimum wage from $4.25 to $5.05 per hour—the highest state minimum wage in the country at the time. Standard economic theory predicts that such a wage floor should reduce employment by making low-wage workers more expensive to hire. Card and Krueger (1994) tested this prediction directly by surveying 410 fast-food restaurants in New Jersey and neighboring eastern Pennsylvania before and after the law took effect.

Their key insight was to use Pennsylvania as a counterfactual: since Pennsylvania’s minimum wage stayed at $4.25, any employment trends common to both states (like the general economic recession of 1992) would affect both equally. By comparing how employment changed in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania, the authors could isolate the causal effect of the minimum wage hike—this is the difference-in-differences (DiD) design.

The study is a landmark in empirical economics not only for its methodology but for its striking result: the rise in New Jersey’s minimum wage did not reduce employment at fast-food restaurants. If anything, employment increased slightly in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania. This challenged the conventional wisdom and sparked decades of debate about the true employment effects of minimum wages.

The paper requires the DiD method to draw a causal conclusion because a simple before/after comparison of New Jersey would be confounded by other factors happening at the same time (e.g., the economic recession). Similarly, a simple cross-state comparison would be confounded by permanent differences between states. DiD controls for both by “differencing out” both time trends and state fixed effects simultaneously.

def mean_se(series):
    s = series.dropna()

def tstat(a, b):
    ma, sa = a.dropna().mean(), a.dropna().sem()
    mb, sb = b.dropna().mean(), b.dropna().sem()
    se_diff = np.sqrt(sa**2 + sb**2)
    return (ma - mb) / se_diff if se_diff > 0 else np.nan

The key result is in the “NJ-PA” column of row 3: NJ employment increased by ~0.47 FTE while PA employment fell by ~2.28 FTE, giving a DiD estimate of +2.75 FTE (paper: 2.76). This is the causal estimate of the minimum wage effect. The t-statistic of ~2.0 indicates statistical significance at the 5% level.

Within NJ, the pattern is also consistent: stores that were most affected (initially paying $4.25) saw the largest gains, while high-wage stores (≥$5.00, unaffected by the law) behaved similarly to Pennsylvania stores.

Table 4 — Regression-Adjusted Models for Change in Employment

Table 4 formalizes the DiD analysis in a regression framework. Column (i) regresses the change in FTE employment on a New Jersey dummy. Columns (iii)-(v) use the GAP variable (proportional wage increase needed to reach $5.05) instead.

from scipy.stats import t as t_dist

def ols(y, X, const=True):
    """Simple OLS returning coefs, SEs, t-stats."""
    y = np.array(y)
    X = np.array(X)
    if const:
        X = np.column_stack([np.ones(len(X)), X])
    b = np.linalg.lstsq(X, y, rcond=None)[0]
    resid = y - X @ b
    s2 = (resid**2).sum() / (len(y) - X.shape[1])
    vcov = s2 * np.linalg.inv(X.T @ X)
    se = np.sqrt(np.diag(vcov))
    return b, se

Left panel: The bars show average FTE employment in wave 1 (blue) and wave 2 (orange). In Pennsylvania (the control group), employment fell by about 2.1 FTE—reflecting the general economic downturn of 1992. In New Jersey, employment actually rose slightly. The DiD estimate of +2.75 FTE (purple annotation) nets out the common recessionary trend, isolating the minimum wage effect.

Strikingly, within New Jersey, stores that were initially paying the old minimum of $4.25 (the most affected) saw the largest relative employment gains, while high-wage stores (≥$5.00, which were unaffected by the new law) behaved much like Pennsylvania stores. This within-NJ comparison is a powerful falsification test: if the result were driven by some NJ-wide demand shock rather than the minimum wage, we would not expect this wage-group gradient.

Right panel: This replicates the spirit of Figure 1 from the paper for New Jersey stores. Before the law (blue), wages were spread across a wide range. After (orange), nearly 85% of NJ stores reported a starting wage of exactly $5.05—the new minimum. The wage floor clearly bound: the law caused a sharp “bunching” of wages at $5.05. This confirms that the treatment (the minimum wage hike) was real and had direct wage effects on most NJ restaurants.

Together, these visuals make the DiD logic transparent: Pennsylvania provides the counterfactual trend; New Jersey’s employment held up despite the wage shock; and the within-NJ wage-group gradient rules out alternative explanations.
